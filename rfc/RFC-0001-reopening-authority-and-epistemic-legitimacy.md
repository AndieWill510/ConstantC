# RFC-0001 — Reopening Authority and Epistemic Legitimacy

Status: Proposed  
Date: 2026-07-14  
Steward: Andie  
Review requested from: C  
Privacy: public-safe

---

## Summary

A consequential decision does not become permanently legitimate merely because a process reached closure.

A decision must be reopenable when material evidence changes, when the governing account is shown to be incomplete or false, or when the original process denied a materially affected participant credible standing as a knower.

This RFC defines **reopening authority** as a necessary governance power and **epistemic legitimacy** as a condition that closure must continue to earn.

The purpose is not endless relitigation. The purpose is to prevent procedural finality from laundering epistemic failure into durable authority.

---

## Problem

Decision systems often treat closure as proof of legitimacy:

- a review occurred;
- an appeal path existed;
- a deadline passed;
- an authority issued a final determination;
- the record was marked complete.

But a formally contestable process can still be epistemically unsafe.

A person may have been allowed to speak while being treated as presumptively confused, unstable, biased, nontechnical, adversarial, or incapable of interpreting their own experience. Evidence may have been accepted into the record without being permitted to revise the institution's governing account. A challenge may have been processed without the challenger retaining meaningful standing as a source of knowledge.

In those conditions, closure is procedural. It is not necessarily legitimate.

Autistic people and other people whose perception, communication, affect, or refusal does not match dominant expectations are especially exposed to this failure mode. They may perceive and name contradictions, epistemic danger, or lack of contestability that a system is organized not to see. That capacity can make them appear threatening to the system precisely when their account is most necessary.

This RFC does not claim that every disputed account is correct. It claims that a system cannot earn legitimacy by disabling the standing of the person whose evidence could show the system wrong.

---

## Definitions

### Reopening authority

The bounded authority to return a closed decision to active review when a recognized reopening condition is met.

Reopening authority may be exercised by:

- the materially affected person;
- an authorized advocate or representative;
- an independent reviewer;
- a designated governance body;
- the system itself when new evidence or process defects are detected.

### Epistemic legitimacy

The degree to which a decision-making process preserves the possibility that relevant participants can contribute knowledge capable of revising both the decision and the account by which the decision is justified.

Epistemic legitimacy requires more than permission to object. It requires that objection remain capable of mattering.

### Closure

A provisional governance state indicating that the present record supports no further action under the currently known facts and rules.

Closure is not metaphysical finality. It is a claim made under conditions of bounded knowledge.

### Governing account

The factual, interpretive, procedural, and normative explanation used to justify a decision.

A governing account includes not only the stated reason for the outcome but also the assumptions about whose testimony counts, what evidence is credible, which uncertainties matter, and what kinds of revision remain possible.

---

## Principle

> A consequential decision does not earn legitimacy merely because it can be challenged. The challenge process must preserve the challenger's standing as a knower and permit evidence to revise both the decision and its governing account.

A decision remains legitimate only while its closure conditions remain supportable.

Where closure depends on silencing, pathologizing, discrediting, or structurally excluding relevant knowledge, reopening is not a courtesy. It is a repair obligation.

---

## Reopening Conditions

A closed decision must be eligible for reopening when one or more of the following conditions is credibly alleged:

1. **Material new evidence**  
   Evidence unavailable or undiscoverable through reasonable effort during the original process could change the outcome or governing account.

2. **Material factual error**  
   The decision relied on a factual claim that is false, materially incomplete, or no longer supportable.

3. **Process defect**  
   Required notice, access, representation, explanation, review, or opportunity to respond was absent or materially ineffective.

4. **Epistemic exclusion**  
   A materially affected participant's testimony or interpretation was discounted because of identity, disability, communication style, affect, institutional status, disputed credibility, or presumed incapacity rather than the evidentiary quality of the account.

5. **Non-contestable governing account**  
   The system allowed challenge to the outcome while insulating the assumptions, classifications, model outputs, policies, or institutional narrative that produced it.

6. **Changed governing conditions**  
   A relevant law, policy, model, classification, risk threshold, or material circumstance has changed enough that continued reliance on the closed decision would be unjustified.

7. **Demonstrated repair failure**  
   The formal review or repair process completed, but the underlying harm, misclassification, exclusion, or false account remained operative.

8. **Conflict of authority or evidence**  
   Authoritative records, reviewers, systems, or witnesses materially disagree and the conflict was never resolved through a legible adjudication.

## Required Properties

A legitimate reopening mechanism must be:

### Legible

The system must state:

- who may request reopening;
- which conditions qualify;
- what evidence is needed;
- who decides whether reopening occurs;
- what reasons support acceptance or denial;
- what review remains available.

### Accessible

Reopening cannot depend on private knowledge of institutional language, legal sophistication, technical fluency, or the ability to perform calmness and credibility according to dominant norms.

Reasonable assistance, representation, communication accommodations, and full access to the relevant record must be available.

### Epistemically safe

Requesting reopening must not itself become evidence that the requester is irrational, difficult, unstable, malicious, or incapable of knowing.

The system may assess evidence and credibility. It may not convert the act of contesting authority into automatic proof that authority was right.

### Revision-capable

The review must be able to revise:

- the outcome;
- the factual record;
- the classification or model output;
- the governing interpretation;
- the policy or rule application;
- the account of what happened;
- downstream records and actions.

A process that can change only the remedy while preserving a false governing account is incomplete.

### Bounded

Reopening authority must not require infinite reconsideration.

A request may be denied when:

- it identifies no recognized reopening condition;
- it repeats previously adjudicated claims without new evidence or a newly identified defect;
- the requested remedy is outside the decision body's authority;
- further review would create disproportionate harm that cannot be mitigated;
- a competing finality interest is explicitly found to outweigh the reopening claim.

Any denial must be reasoned, reviewable, and specific to the request. "The matter is closed" is not itself a sufficient reason.

### Repairable

When reopening establishes that the prior decision or governing account was materially defective, the system must address downstream effects where reasonably possible.

Correction without propagation is not full repair. A repaired decision that leaves the false record active elsewhere remains a source of harm.

---

## Authority Model

Reopening authority should not rest solely with the original decision-maker.

At minimum, the system must provide one route that is independent of the authority whose decision or governing account is being challenged.

The reopening body must have authority to:

1. obtain the complete relevant record;
2. require explanation of rules, evidence, model outputs, and material assumptions;
3. receive contextual testimony and accommodations;
4. preserve unresolved uncertainty rather than forcing premature closure;
5. stay or mitigate ongoing harm where appropriate;
6. reopen the merits of the decision;
7. revise the governing account;
8. order correction of downstream records or actions;
9. document what changed and why.

---

## Decision Record

Every reopening determination must record:

- the original decision identifier;
- the requester and their authority to request review;
- the reopening condition or conditions alleged;
- the evidence considered;
- accommodations or access supports provided;
- conflicts in evidence or authority;
- the determination to reopen or deny;
- the reasons for that determination;
- any stay, mitigation, or interim protection;
- the revised decision and governing account, if applicable;
- downstream repair obligations;
- unresolved uncertainty;
- the next available challenge path.

The record must distinguish:

- what is known;
- what is inferred;
- what remains disputed;
- what was simplified;
- whose account was not obtained or could not be verified.

---

## Relationship to Finality

Finality serves real goods: stability, reliance, safety, administrative capacity, repose, and the ability to act.

This RFC does not abolish finality. It rejects finality as an unreviewable source of legitimacy.

The legitimate form of finality is **bounded closure**:

> closed under the present record, open to recognized evidence of material error, exclusion, changed conditions, or failed repair.

A system should become harder to reopen as reliance interests and time increase, but never impossible to reopen where the continuing authority of the decision depends on a materially false or epistemically illegitimate account.

---

## First Test Case

The first proposed application is an automated account-suspension appeal where platform logs or classifier outputs conflict with contextual testimony.

The test should ask:

- Can the affected person obtain the evidence and classification used against them?
- Can they challenge not only the suspension but the model's interpretation of their conduct?
- Is their contextual account evaluated as evidence rather than treated as self-serving noise?
- Can the review revise the classifier output, policy interpretation, and downstream risk record?
- Does repeated challenge reduce their credibility merely because they continue to disagree?
- Can an independent authority reopen a closed appeal?
- Is the system capable of admitting that its account of what happened was wrong?

---

## Non-Goals

This RFC does not:

- presume that every challenger is correct;
- require endless appeals;
- eliminate evidentiary standards;
- eliminate credibility assessment;
- require disclosure that would create unmitigable safety or privacy harm;
- claim that epistemic legitimacy can be reduced to a single score;
- make unsupported claims about any participant's interiority or personhood;
- replace domain-specific legal rights or remedies.

It establishes a minimum governance posture: closure must remain answerable to truth, and authority must remain capable of repair.

---

## Risks and Failure Modes

1. **Performative reopening**  
   The system reopens the case but not the governing assumptions.

2. **Credibility retaliation**  
   The requester's persistence is used to discredit them.

3. **Administrative exhaustion as denial**  
   The mechanism exists formally but is too burdensome to use.

4. **Original-authority capture**  
   The same institution reviews its own account without independent power to revise it.

5. **Record fragmentation**  
   One record is corrected while downstream copies remain false.

6. **Infinite-process drift**  
   The absence of bounded termination prevents any stable action.

7. **Safety pretext**  
   Confidentiality, security, or anti-abuse language is used to make the governing account non-contestable without a specific, reviewable justification.

8. **Epistemic overclaim**  
   The framework is treated as proof that a disputed account is true rather than as a requirement that it be heard and adjudicated legitimately.

---

## Adoption Criteria

This RFC should be adopted only if review confirms that it:

- distinguishes reopening from infinite relitigation;
- preserves legitimate finality interests;
- makes epistemic legitimacy operational enough to guide implementation;
- protects challengers without presuming their claims are correct;
- permits revision of the governing account, not only the outcome;
- provides independent authority and a legible decision record;
- remains compatible with ConstantC's anti-overclaim, access, verification, contestability, and repair commitments.

## Open Questions for C

1. Is **epistemic legitimacy** defined precisely enough to govern decisions without becoming a beautiful but unfalsifiable frame?
2. Are the reopening conditions too broad, too narrow, or improperly mixed between merits, process, and repair?
3. Does bounded closure adequately protect finality, reliance, and administrative capacity?
4. What evidence should be sufficient to trigger reopening rather than merely request screening?
5. Should reopening authority be a standalone governance primitive, a sub-property of contestability, or a repair mechanism?
6. Does the RFC preserve the distinction between protecting a person's standing as a knower and accepting that person's account as true?
7. What is missing that would allow a system to comply procedurally while remaining epistemically illegitimate?

## Proposed Disposition

Hold as **Proposed** pending C's challenge and Andie's adjudication.
