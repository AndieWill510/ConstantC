## Why this case exists

`Standing Is the First Covenant` claims that standing may be a constitutional primitive rather than a poetic restatement of contestability, complexity tolerance, or legitimacy.

That claim has not earned independent status merely because it is resonant.

It needs a case in which:

- the system is legible;
- the system is formally contestable;
- the system preserves provenance;
- the system includes human review;
- the system tolerates relevant complexity at least in form;
- and yet a materially affected participant still lacks sufficient standing to alter the shared account.

AI-assisted prior authorization for public health coverage supplies such a case.

---

## Current policy context and bounded claim

This worked case is not invented from nowhere.

Beginning in 2026, the federal Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services began testing the Wasteful and Inappropriate Service Reduction, or WISeR, model in six states. The model applies prior authorization to selected services under Original Medicare and uses enhanced technology, including AI and machine learning, to support review. Public reporting states that licensed clinicians make final adverse determinations and that appeal rights remain available.

That factual context matters because it demonstrates that AI-supported prior authorization in a federal public-health program is already an operating governance problem, not a remote hypothetical.

This note does **not** claim, without further evidence, that Medicare and Medicaid have been fully nationalized or that state authority has already been categorically displaced. Medicare is already federally administered; Medicaid remains a federal-state program with substantial state administration. Broader claims about federal consolidation of Medicaid authority require a separate evidence record.

The narrower claim is enough:

> A federally governed, AI-assisted prior-authorization system can preserve formal appeal rights while still denying some affected people meaningful standing inside the inquiry.

Public reporting used for this bounded context:

- https://time.com/7313351/medicare-prior-authorization-pilot-states-concerns/
- https://www.kiplinger.com/retirement/medicare/prior-authorization-coming-to-traditional-medicare
- https://www.marketwatch.com/story/a-new-medicare-program-that-uses-ai-for-prior-authorizations-is-hurting-patients-and-delaying-care-9e25b98b

These sources establish current policy context and reported concerns. They do not establish the hypothetical discrimination pattern developed below.

## The system

Imagine a CDP-governed prior-authorization system used for a public-health benefit.

The system appears exemplary by conventional governance standards.

Every denial is explainable.

Every decision preserves provenance.

Every policy rule and model version is recorded.

Every request can be appealed.

Human reviewers remain available.

Bias monitoring exists.

Audit trails exist.

A final adverse decision must be made by an authorized clinician.

The system accepts narrative evidence, clinician documentation, structured records, relevant accommodations, and case-specific context.

It is legible.

It is formally contestable.

It is repairable in design.

It claims to tolerate complexity.

No one is explicitly forbidden to speak.

## Two materially similar appeals

Consider two beneficiaries seeking authorization for the same covered treatment.

Their diagnoses, treatment histories, functional limitations, prior therapies, and medical risks are materially similar.

### Appeal A

The first beneficiary is autistic.

She describes pain, impairment, sensory consequences, executive-function loss, and the effect of delayed treatment in language that does not resemble the communication patterns the model has learned to treat as medically persuasive.

Her account may be nonlinear.

It may include unusually precise observations alongside difficulty naming common symptoms.

Her distress may be visible.

She may emphasize lived consequences that are clinically relevant but not expressed in the administrative vocabulary expected by the model.

Her physician supports the request and supplies appropriate clinical evidence.

The system records everything.

The appeal channel functions.

A human reviewer reads the record.

Yet the beneficiary's own testimony is consistently assigned low evidentiary weight because it is treated as atypical, emotionally excessive, insufficiently clinical, internally inconsistent, or unreliable by category.

The system allows her to speak.

It does not allow what she says to matter proportionately.

### Appeal B

The second beneficiary presents materially similar clinical facts.

Her physician and advocate translate the same lived consequences into language already recognized by the model and reviewers as authoritative, orderly, calm, and medically persuasive.

The relevant facts have not materially changed.

The communication has.

The second appeal is approved.

---

## What did not change

The formal appeal right did not change.

The provenance record did not change.

The existence of human review did not change.

The evidence-submission mechanism did not change.

The nominal complexity-tolerance policy did not change.

The repair pathway did not change.

The authority of the final reviewer did not change.

Only one thing changed:

> The system recognized one beneficiary's account as capable of altering the inquiry and treated the other's as administratively present but epistemically optional.

That is a difference in standing.

---

## Why contestability is insufficient

Contestability asks:

> Can the decision be challenged through a legitimate mechanism?

In this case, yes.

Both beneficiaries can appeal.

Both can submit evidence.

Both can receive review.

Both can obtain a reasoned decision.

The system is contestable in form.

Standing asks a different question:

> Can this participant's contribution meaningfully change the shared account?

For Appeal A, the honest answer may be no.

The participant is recognized as a source only ceremonially. Her testimony enters the record but cannot acquire enough weight to revise the controlling interpretation.

A mechanism of challenge exists.

The challenger has not been preserved as a meaningful participant in truth.

This is relational epistemic unsafety.

---

## Why complexity tolerance is insufficient

The system may claim to tolerate complex cases.

It may accept long narratives, disability accommodations, multiple diagnoses, contradictory records, atypical presentation, and clinician supplementation.

That still does not prove that the participant herself retains standing.

A system can tolerate complexity as data while discounting the complex person as a knower.

It can ingest the narrative while treating the narrator as unreliable by default.

It can preserve the edge case while refusing to let the person at the edge revise institutional belief.

Complexity tolerance asks whether the system can hold the case.

Standing asks whether the participant can matter inside it.

Those are not the same property.

---

## The irreducibility test

This case supplies a concrete test for whether standing performs independent work.

A system preserves standing only if it can answer:

> **Describe one plausible contribution this participant could make that would be capable of changing the outcome or the controlling account.**

The contribution may be:

- new clinical evidence;
- correction of a factual error;
- relevant lived testimony;
- evidence that the policy was misapplied;
- evidence that the model misunderstood atypical communication;
- evidence that the administrative record omitted material context;
- evidence that a credibility discount arose from disability, distress, language, fluency, status, or source category rather than evidentiary quality.

The test does not require the participant to prevail.

It requires that some relevant contribution available to that participant could matter in principle and in operation.

If the honest answer is:

> Nothing this person says, clarifies, experiences, or submits in her own capacity could ever alter the account,

then the appeal is ceremonial.

The system remains contestable in form while failing relational epistemic safety in substance.

## Sufficient, not identical standing

Standing does not require every participant to have identical authority.

The beneficiary does not become the final clinical adjudicator.

The model does not become a moral authority.

The physician does not gain unlimited power to compel payment.

The reviewing clinician remains responsible for applying law, coverage policy, evidence, and professional judgment.

Standing is sufficient when each participant has a defined contribution that can matter within the scope of that participant's role.

For this case:

- the beneficiary has standing concerning lived effects, functional impairment, chronology, preferences, consent, and errors in how her account is represented;
- the treating clinician has standing concerning diagnosis, medical necessity, treatment history, prognosis, and clinical risk;
- the reviewing clinician has adjudicative authority bounded by policy, evidence, conflict rules, and reviewability;
- the model has no independent moral standing and no unreviewable authority, but its outputs have consequential force and therefore require provenance, testing, challenge, and accountable interpretation;
- the agency has policy authority but cannot treat its own records or classifications as infallible;
- the public has standing concerning lawful use of public funds, equity, aggregate outcomes, and whether the program's incentives distort care.

A checkable threshold follows:

> For every participant, name the domain in which that participant's contribution is relevant, the mechanism by which it enters the record, the conditions under which it may change the account, and the authority that may reject it with reasons.

If those four elements cannot be named, `sufficient standing` remains rhetoric.

## Consent

Prior authorization is not ordinarily a consensual inquiry in the ordinary interpersonal sense.

The beneficiary cannot simply decline the process without risking loss of coverage or care.

That asymmetry makes consent more important, not less.

Real consent in this context does not mean consenting to whether public funds are governed or whether coverage rules exist.

It means that the beneficiary must be able to know and contest:

- that automated or algorithmic tools are materially involved;
- what role those tools play;
- what data and testimony are used;
- how credibility and medical necessity are evaluated;
- who makes the final decision;
- what rights of review, correction, accommodation, representation, and appeal exist;
- what downstream records, scores, labels, or future decisions may be affected;
- how a successful challenge repairs both the immediate outcome and the institutional record.

Where refusal is not realistically available, procedural knowledge, representation, correction, and contestability become minimum conditions of legitimate participation.

Consent without standing is a formality.

Standing without meaningful disclosure is blind participation.

Both are insufficient.

## CDP implementation seam

ConstantC names the constitutional obligation.

CDP supplies the governed decision path.

A CDP implementation for this case should preserve at least:

### Propose

- requested treatment;
- coverage rule invoked;
- model-supported recommendation;
- evidence supplied by each source;
- known accommodations and communication needs;
- declared uncertainty;
- participant-role map.

### Challenge

- beneficiary challenge;
- clinician challenge;
- challenge to factual accuracy;
- challenge to policy application;
- challenge to model interpretation;
- challenge to credibility discounts;
- challenge to missing context or inaccessible procedure.

### Test

- compare materially similar cases;
- test whether communication style changes evidentiary weight;
- test whether disability, distress, fluency, language, race, gender, age, or source status alters outcomes after relevant clinical differences are controlled;
- test model and reviewer consistency;
- test whether the participant's plausible contribution can actually revise the account.

### Adjudicate

- identify the authorized adjudicator;
- disclose conflicts and incentives;
- distinguish credibility, evidence quality, standing, correctness, and authority;
- state why each material contribution was accepted, limited, or rejected;
- record what would have changed the result.

### Legitimize

- confirm lawful authority;
- confirm that required participants retained sufficient standing;
- confirm that accommodations were effective rather than merely offered;
- confirm that the outcome survived challenge under known standards;
- distinguish final-for-process from infallible-in-truth.

### Execute

- approve, deny, modify, or request additional information;
- communicate the decision accessibly;
- prevent execution while a timely challenge with serious risk remains unresolved where law and clinical safety permit.

### Record

- preserve evidence and provenance;
- preserve credibility rationales;
- preserve standing determinations;
- preserve unresolved uncertainty;
- preserve model version, policy version, reviewer identity, authority scope, and material incentives.

### Learn and repair

- correct the immediate decision when warranted;
- repair linked records, risk labels, enforcement histories, and future model inputs;
- measure reversal, abandonment, delay, and harm rates;
- examine patterns by disability, language, distress, communication style, source role, and access to representation;
- revise policy, model, workflow, and training when standing failures recur.

CDP does not manufacture standing by creating more procedure.

It operationalizes standing only when the procedure remains capable of changing institutional belief.

## Adjudication and bounded withdrawal

Standing is not irrevocable authority.

A participant may harass, fabricate evidence, repeat a finally adjudicated claim without material new information, or use procedure to impose unacceptable harm on others.

The system may narrow participation, close a process, or reject a claim.

But withdrawal or limitation of standing must itself be governed.

The record should state:

- what conduct or evidentiary failure justified the limitation;
- whether the limitation applies to a claim, channel, role, period, or person;
- who adjudicated it;
- what less restrictive measures were considered;
- what evidence could alter the limitation;
- what new evidence or procedural defect permits reopening;
- how other affected participants are protected.

The same body that made the original decision should not possess unlimited, unreviewable authority to decide whether every future challenge is bad-faith relitigation.

Where stakes are serious, an independent or differently situated reviewer should evaluate disputes about whether standing was legitimately limited or illegitimately foreclosed.

This is where the first covenant must hand off to governed adjudication rather than attempting to solve every boundary through culture alone.

## What this case demonstrates

This case does not prove that standing is the final or highest primitive.

It does demonstrate that formal contestability and stated complexity tolerance can coexist with categorical credibility discounting.

A person can be allowed to appeal while remaining unable to matter.

A system can hold complex data while refusing to recognize the complex person as a source of knowledge.

A human reviewer can remain in the loop while inheriting the model's credibility hierarchy.

A complete audit trail can preserve a procedurally immaculate record of epistemic exclusion.

Standing therefore appears to perform work not exhausted by the mere existence of challenge, complexity handling, human review, provenance, or repair procedure.

The case supports the candidate architecture:

- **Standing** is the candidate constitutional primitive.
- **Relational epistemic safety** is the property of an inquiry that preserves sufficient standing among participants.
- **Contestability** is one mechanism through which standing is exercised.
- **Complexity tolerance** allows the system to hold difficult cases without discarding them.
- **Repair** restores outcomes, records, and standing after failure.
- **CDP** supplies the governed path by which those obligations become operational, bounded, and auditable.

The case also establishes a practical threshold:

> For every materially affected participant, identify one plausible contribution they can make that is capable of changing the controlling account, and show the governed path by which it can do so.

If the system cannot do that, the participant has access to procedure without standing in inquiry.

---

## Closing

An appeal channel is not enough.

A human reviewer is not enough.

An audit trail is not enough.

The ability to submit testimony is not enough.

A system becomes relationally epistemically safer only when the people who bear its consequences retain sufficient standing for what they reveal to alter what the system believes.

Standing is the first covenant because inquiry cannot discover what it has already forbidden itself to hear.

In public-health governance, that sentence becomes operational:

> **Describe one plausible contribution this beneficiary could make that would change the account.**

When the answer is `none`, the system has not completed an inquiry.

It has completed a denial.
