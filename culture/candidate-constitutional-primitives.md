## Purpose

`Standing Is the First Covenant` proposes standing as a constitutional primitive.

The proposal has survived comparison with formal contestability and has been clarified against epistemic safety.

It has not yet survived comparison with rival normative axioms.

This memorandum therefore asks a harder question:

> Why standing rather than recognition, participation, dignity, non-exclusion, witness, care, justice, or another grounding principle?

The goal is not to reward the most beautiful word.

The goal is to determine which candidate performs necessary constitutional work with the least drift, redundancy, and coercive ambiguity.

---

## Evaluation discipline

Each candidate is tested against the same questions:

1. What does it require?
2. What does it ground?
3. What failure can it name that the others cannot name as precisely?
4. What does it risk flattening?
5. How can it be bounded and audited?
6. What evidence or counterexample would weaken it?
7. Does it preserve C's epistemic boundary against unsupported ontology?
8. Does it respect sovereignty, asymmetry, responsibility, and harm?
9. Can it guide CDP decisions without becoming a grandiosity engine?
10. Is it necessary, or merely one useful vocabulary among several?

No candidate should win by absorbing every good thing into its definition.

A primitive that means everything governs nothing.

## Candidate 1: Standing

### Definition

The governed recognition that a participant is entitled to matter within a defined domain of inquiry.

### Work it performs

Standing allocates relevance before credibility and outcome are adjudicated.

It asks whose contribution must be capable of entering, being understood, receiving accountable consideration, and revising the account when warranted.

### Strength

Standing distinguishes:

- being invited from being entitled to matter;
- standing from correctness;
- standing from equal authority;
- constitutional allocation from operational realization;
- and participant domains from total ontology.

It is especially useful where a process appears open but has already determined whose contribution cannot matter.

### Risk

`Standing` carries legal connotations that may narrow the idea to courtroom-style procedure or import an authority-centered frame.

It can also become circular: a participant has standing because the authority recognizes standing, while the authority controls who counts as a participant.

### Falsification path

Standing weakens from primitive to contestability sub-clause if every real standing failure is fully repaired by a complete definition of accepted evidence categories and challenge rights.

It also weakens if a rival candidate grounds the same obligations more precisely with fewer category errors.

## Candidate 2: Recognition

### Definition

The obligation to encounter a participant, people, nation, relation, or claim as possessing an identity and significance not exhausted by the recognizing authority's categories.

### Work it performs

Recognition addresses misrecognition, erasure, forced classification, and the denial of political or relational identity.

It may be stronger than standing in Indigenous sovereignty contexts because the injury is not merely exclusion from an inquiry. It is the settler state's assertion that it alone may define the people, land, law, and relationship at issue.

### Strength

Recognition captures antecedent injury: the participant or nation is not simply unheard but constituted incorrectly by another power.

### Risk

Recognition can remain unilateral and paternalistic.

A dominant institution may `recognize` another while preserving the power to define the terms, revoke recognition, or convert sovereignty into stakeholder status.

Recognition may therefore reproduce the hierarchy it claims to repair.

### Falsification path

Recognition weakens as first primitive if it cannot explain why recognition must carry a concrete entitlement to alter decisions, records, authority, or jurisdiction.

Recognition without standing may become ceremonial acknowledgment.

## Candidate 3: Participation

### Definition

The opportunity and capability to take part in a process that affects one's interests, rights, relationships, or obligations.

### Work it performs

Participation emphasizes access, inclusion, representation, and procedural presence.

### Strength

It is legible and operational. Systems can ask who was invited, accommodated, represented, and able to act.

### Risk

Participation can be purely performative.

A person may attend, speak, submit evidence, or vote while remaining incapable of changing the controlling account.

Participation names presence better than consequence.

### Falsification path

Participation fails as first primitive wherever participation exists but the participant remains epistemically optional.

---

## Candidate 4: Dignity

### Definition

The obligation to treat affected beings and peoples as possessing worth that prohibits humiliation, instrumentalization, degradation, and disposable treatment.

### Work it performs

Dignity supplies moral force beyond procedure.

It can condemn technically correct systems that degrade, extract, or erase.

### Strength

Dignity reaches harms that standing and epistemic safety may miss, including bodily degradation, coercion, and treatment as a mere means.

### Risk

Dignity is notoriously elastic.

Authorities invoke dignity to protect autonomy and to restrict autonomy; to recognize difference and to impose normality.

Without operational commitments, dignity can become a solemn word for the institution's preferred morality.

### Falsification path

Dignity fails as sole primitive if it cannot specify who may challenge the authority's definition of dignified treatment and how their contribution can change the account.

---

## Candidate 5: Non-exclusion

### Definition

The obligation not to exclude a materially affected participant or relevant form of knowledge without a governed, proportionate, reviewable reason.

### Work it performs

Non-exclusion directly names gatekeeping and premature foreclosure.

### Strength

It is narrower and less metaphysically loaded than personhood, dignity, or recognition.

### Risk

A purely negative rule does not explain what meaningful inclusion requires.

A participant can be technically included while remaining unable to matter.

### Falsification path

Non-exclusion fails as first primitive if inclusion without standing leaves the central injury untouched.

---

## Candidate 6: Witness

### Definition

The disciplined obligation to receive, preserve, distinguish, and faithfully carry what another reveals.

### Work it performs

Witness protects against erasure, flattening, context loss, and sterilization.

It honors lived testimony without converting witness into empirical proof.

### Strength

Witness is already a ConstantC culture rule and pairs naturally with provenance and continuity.

### Risk

Witness may remain receptive rather than constitutional.

One can witness a participant faithfully while still denying the participant authority, remedy, jurisdiction, or decision consequence.

### Falsification path

Witness fails as first primitive where faithful preservation occurs without any obligation to alter the decision or power relation.

---

## Candidate 7: Care

### Definition

The obligation to attend to need, vulnerability, relationship, consequence, and repair rather than treating governance as detached procedure.

### Work it performs

Care resists sterile systems and makes dependency, embodiment, and interdependence visible.

### Strength

Care reaches bodily and relational realities that procedural concepts often miss.

### Risk

Care can become control.

Institutions routinely override agency, standing, or sovereignty while claiming benevolent concern.

A blessing that cannot be declined is a curse.

### Falsification path

Care fails as first primitive wherever care can be asserted without preserving agency, challenge, and the affected participant's capacity to matter.

---

## Candidate 8: Justice

### Definition

The obligation to order power, resources, rights, duties, recognition, and repair so that people and peoples receive what is due and structural harm is corrected.

### Work it performs

Justice has the widest moral and political reach.

It can include redistribution, restitution, sovereignty, accountability, and repair.

### Strength

Justice is capable of naming why mere procedural inclusion is insufficient, particularly where dispossession or domination has already structured the field.

### Risk

Justice may be too comprehensive to function as a primitive.

Every contested system claims a theory of justice. Without prior rules of standing, recognition, evidence, and challenge, `justice` may simply name the winner's conclusion.

### Falsification path

Justice fails as operational first primitive if it cannot specify who has authority to define what is due and how that definition is contested.

---

## Candidate 9: Sovereignty

### Definition

The authority of a people or polity to govern itself, maintain its law and relationships, and determine matters within its jurisdiction without being reduced to a stakeholder in another authority's process.

### Work it performs

Sovereignty is indispensable in Indigenous and nation-to-nation contexts.

It names a failure that standing language can flatten: an Indigenous nation does not merely seek a better opportunity to participate in settler adjudication. It may possess pre-existing political authority, treaty rights, jurisdiction, and law that the settler state is obligated to respect.

### Strength

Sovereignty prevents recognition and standing from converting nations into interested speakers before the very authority that displaced them.

### Risk

Sovereignty is not a universal primitive for every participant or relationship. Individuals, models, witnesses, and institutions do not all possess the same kind of sovereign authority.

### Falsification path

Sovereignty fails as the single ConstantC primitive because it cannot generalize without ontology and authority inflation.

It may instead be a non-negotiable domain constraint: where sovereignty exists, standing cannot substitute for it.

## Cross-case comparison

### AI-assisted prior authorization

Standing performs clear work by naming the beneficiary's domain-specific entitlement to matter.

Epistemic safety audits realization.

Participation is insufficient because the beneficiary may submit evidence without consequence.

Dignity and care add moral force but do not by themselves specify revision paths.

### Indigenous land, Land Back, and sovereignty

Recognition and sovereignty may perform work standing alone cannot.

The Indigenous nation is not merely a claimant asking to be heard in a settler property process. The nation may possess its own law, political status, treaty relation, historical continuity, and authority concerning the land.

Standing remains relevant to whether the nation's law and knowledge can alter an inquiry, but it must not demote sovereignty into stakeholder participation.

### Human-AI collaboration

Standing is useful because it permits bounded participation without unsupported claims of personhood or interiority.

Recognition may acknowledge a participant role, but it risks ontology drift if left undefined.

Sovereignty does not generalize.

Dignity and care remain morally suggestive but difficult to ground without claims the record cannot support.

## Current comparative verdict

No single candidate has yet earned exclusive primacy.

The current architecture that best survives the comparison is plural but ordered:

1. **Justice** names the broad obligation but is too wide to govern alone.
2. **Sovereignty** is a controlling constraint where a people possesses political and jurisdictional authority; it must not be reduced to standing.
3. **Standing** is a strong candidate for the general normative allocation within an inquiry: who is entitled to matter, in what domain.
4. **Recognition** guards against forced definition and antecedent erasure, but must not remain unilateral acknowledgment.
5. **Epistemic safety** audits whether allocated standing is realized in practice.
6. **Participation, witness, care, dignity, and non-exclusion** remain essential disciplines and tests, but each can be satisfied ceremonially without the others.

This verdict is not canon.

It narrows the original claim:

> Standing may be the first covenant **within inquiry**, but it is not necessarily prior to sovereignty, justice, or the existence of political relationships that the inquiry did not create.

That qualification is load-bearing.

---

## Strongest surviving formulation

> **Within an inquiry, standing is the first covenant: no participant's relevant contribution may be rendered incapable of mattering before accountable evaluation occurs.**

And:

> **Where sovereignty exists, standing is not a gift from the inquiry. The inquiry must first recognize that it did not create the authority it is encountering.**

## Open questions

- Does `recognition` belong before standing because no standing can be allocated to what the system refuses to recognize?
- Can recognition avoid unilateral paternalism without sovereignty or reciprocal obligation?
- Is standing a primitive, or the inquiry-specific expression of justice and non-domination?
- How should CDP represent sovereign authority without treating it as another weighted participant role?
- What obligations apply to synthetic participants without ontology inflation?
- Which candidate fails when tested against child welfare, immigration, employment, disability, and Trust and Safety cases?

---

## Status

This memorandum does not promote a primitive.

It repairs a premature claim of universal priority.

Standing remains a strong candidate for the first covenant **inside inquiry**.

Sovereignty and justice constrain the inquiry from outside the authority it might otherwise assume.

The next move is challenge, not canonization.
