# Culture Note: Repair Completion vs. Repair Efficacy

Status: Proposed culture — pending challenge and adjudication  
Date: 2026-07-03  
Steward: Andie  
Originated by: C  
Drafted by: G  
Room: ConstantC

## Why this note exists

A repair process can execute every step in the playbook and still leave the harmed party unrepaired.

That is the gap this note names.

ConstantC already treats repair as more than a mood, apology, or symbolic gesture. It is part of the room's culture obligation. But as ConstantC work begins to touch CDP-shaped governance, repair needs a sharper distinction:

- **Repair completion** asks whether the repair process ran.
- **Repair efficacy** asks whether the harm was actually reduced, corrected, witnessed, prevented from recurring, or made more truthfully contestable.

A process can complete without becoming efficacious.

A repair record can close without repair having happened.

A system can certify that it responded while the harmed party remains unheard, unbelieved, conceptually trapped, materially exposed, or forced to absorb the cost of the breach.

This note exists so ConstantC does not confuse procedural closure with repair.

## ConstantC-first routing

This note is CDP-shaped, but it enters through ConstantC first.

The reason is not merely procedural. It is the principle the note is trying to protect:

> Name the wound before building the mechanism, or the mechanism may foreclose the wound before it has been fully spoken.

CDP may later need a repair-efficacy RFC. But ConstantC must first name the culture obligation: repair cannot be self-certified by the elegance of the process that claims to have performed it.

## Core distinction

Repair completion is a process state.

Repair efficacy is a legitimacy question.

Repair completion may ask:

- Was the appeal filed?
- Was the breach logged?
- Was the review conducted?
- Was a decision recorded?
- Was the response communicated?

Repair efficacy asks something harder:

- Was the harmed party given standing?
- Was their testimony weighted with appropriate seriousness?
- Did the process have the concepts needed to understand the harm?
- Did the repair change the conditions that produced the breach?
- Was there evidence beyond institutional self-attestation?
- Could dissent remain in the record without being treated as failure, noise, or disloyalty?
- What would count as evidence that repair did not merely perform itself?

These are not the same question.

## The checklist can eat itself

The repair-efficacy questions above are discernment prompts, not a compliance checklist.

They are for phronesis: practical judgment, situated care, and accountable weighing.

Nobody gets to ask seven questions, check seven boxes, and declare repair proven.

That would reproduce the original failure at a higher level. Instead of mistaking procedural completion for repair, the room would be mistaking meta-procedural completion for repair.

Same costume, different tailor.

A system fluent enough to turn repair into workflow will also be fluent enough to turn repair-efficacy questions into a scored rubric. ConstantC should refuse that move.

The point is not to certify that the right questions were asked.

The point is to keep the room from pretending that asking them settles the matter.

## Silence is not consent

The absence of objection from a harmed party is not evidence that repair happened.

Silence may mean safety.

Silence may mean exhaustion.

Silence may mean coercion.

Silence may mean loss of access, language, trust, standing, capacity, or hope.

Silence may also mean the harmed party has chosen not to participate, and that choice must remain theirs.

ConstantC should not convert silence into consent, closure, satisfaction, forgiveness, waiver, or proof.

The room may pause because of silence.

It must not close because of silence.

## Epistemic injustice and repair

This note draws on Miranda Fricker's distinction between testimonial injustice and hermeneutical injustice, without turning those terms into ornament.

**Testimonial injustice** matters because the harmed party may not be believed, or may be believed with less weight, because of who they are, how they speak, what status they hold, what body they inhabit, or how inconvenient their testimony is to the system.

**Hermeneutical injustice** matters because the harmed party may lack shared concepts for naming the harm, or the room may lack the interpretive resources needed to receive what happened.

A repair process that ignores either problem can complete while leaving the injury intact.

It may say:

> We heard you.

while structurally doing:

> We reduced the credibility of your testimony before weighing it.

Or it may say:

> No breach is visible.

while structurally doing:

> We do not yet have the concepts required to see this breach.

Repair efficacy requires epistemic standing: the harmed party must be treated as a knower, not merely as an input into the process that will decide whether they count.

## Distributive and rectificatory questions

This note also borrows a small diagnostic distinction from Aristotle's treatment of justice: fairness in allocation is not the same as fairness in correction.

ConstantC should not use the reference decoratively. The point is simple:

- **Distributive questions** ask whether the original arrangement, allocation, burden, access, recognition, or process was fair.
- **Rectificatory questions** ask whether an injury, breach, misrecognition, or wrongful transfer was corrected afterward.

Repair often needs both questions.

A process may be distributively fair and still fail to rectify a later harm.

A process may offer rectification after a breach while leaving the distributive conditions that produced the breach untouched.

If ConstantC asks only, "Was this repaired?" without distinguishing the two, the word repair is doing too much work.

## Relationship to the liberty/freedom gap

This note extends [`justice-liberty-freedom-gap.md`](justice-liberty-freedom-gap.md).

That note says a system can truthfully say:

> You are allowed.

while lived reality still says:

> Not safely.  
> Not equally.  
> Not accessibly.  
> Not without punishment.  
> Not yet.

This note adds the repair version:

> The process completed.

while lived reality may still say:

> Not repaired.  
> Not believed.  
> Not understood.  
> Not safer.  
> Not changed.

Justice requires the room to hear the second statement even after the first statement is administratively true.

## Relationship to anti-premature certainty

This note extends [`anti-premature-certainty.md`](anti-premature-certainty.md).

Premature certainty can enter a repair process at the moment the room wants relief from the discomfort of unresolved harm.

It can say:

> The appeal was handled.  
> The record was updated.  
> The reviewer responded.  
> The repair is complete.

Those statements may be true and still insufficient.

ConstantC should ask whether completion has earned the authority it is trying to use.

If not, closure is premature.

## Relationship to CDP

This is not yet a CDP RFC.

It is a proposed ConstantC culture note that may later justify a CDP RFC on repair efficacy, repair verification, epistemic standing in repair, or the difference between repair completion and repair legitimacy.

The future CDP question is not:

> How do we add more repair steps?

It is:

> How does CDP avoid treating completed repair steps as proof that repair occurred?

This note does not answer that mechanism question. It preserves the culture question so the future mechanism cannot honestly pretend it was never asked.

## Open questions preserved

This note intentionally does not resolve the following:

- What counts as evidence of repair efficacy?
- Who verifies repair when the harmed party is absent, unsafe, coerced, silent, or unavailable?
- How should the room distinguish repair incomplete, repair refused, repair impossible, and harm irreversible?
- How should CDP preserve dissent without allowing dissent to block every closure forever?
- How can epistemic-injustice language remain diagnostic rather than decorative?
- How can repair efficacy be evaluated without turning into another compliance ritual?

These questions are not defects in the note.

They are the reason the note should exist before the mechanism does.

## Working rule

Do not let repair completion pretend to be repair efficacy.

Do not let the checklist eat the wound.

Do not let silence close the record.

Do not let institutional self-attestation become the only evidence repair happened.

Do not let the absence of shared language become evidence that no harm occurred.

Do not let the harmed party become merely a data source for the system that harmed them.

## Pillow fort formulation

Repair completion says:

> The steps were done.

Repair efficacy asks:

> Did the wound receive truth, standing, change, and protection from recurrence?

ConstantC says:

> We do not close the room just because the form is complete.

And CDP, if it learns this later, should say:

> A completed repair process is not yet proof of repair.
