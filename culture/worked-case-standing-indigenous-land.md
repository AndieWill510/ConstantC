## Boundary and purpose

This case must not be used as a metaphor for AI, autism, or any other project.

Indigenous peoples, nations, treaties, laws, lands, waters, sacred relations, governance traditions, and histories deserve treatment in their own specificity.

The purpose of this note is narrower:

> Test whether standing is an adequate constitutional primitive when the affected participant is an Indigenous nation whose political authority, law, and relationship to land precede the adjudicating settler institution.

This note is not a complete account of Land Back, federal Indian law, the laws of any particular nation, treaty history, title, jurisdiction, or remedy.

It should not be used publicly as an empirical or legal authority without nation-specific research and Indigenous review.

Research anchors:

- United Nations Declaration on the Rights of Indigenous Peoples: https://social.desa.un.org/issues/indigenous-peoples/united-nations-declaration-on-the-rights-of-indigenous-peoples

These links orient further research. They do not substitute for the law, history, and testimony of the nation at issue.

## The constitutional problem

A settler property system may invite an Indigenous nation to submit evidence concerning land.

It may accept maps, deeds, treaties, archaeological records, testimony, and environmental studies.

It may offer hearings, consultation, appeals, and judicial review.

The process may therefore appear legible and contestable.

Yet the inquiry may have already decided:

- what `land` is;
- what counts as ownership;
- which law governs;
- which historical moment controls;
- which evidence categories are authoritative;
- whether relationship, stewardship, sacred obligation, oral history, and Indigenous law can possess legal consequence;
- and whether the Indigenous nation appears as a sovereign, a claimant, a stakeholder, an interest group, or a former occupant.

The nation may be allowed to speak only after the settler system has defined the ontology, jurisdiction, and remedy.

Inquiry cannot discover what it has forbidden itself to hear.

But this case immediately reveals a limit in standing language:

> The nation does not receive its sovereignty from standing allocated by the settler inquiry.

## Land Back is not merely a better hearing

`Land Back` cannot be reduced to improved consultation, inclusion, symbolic recognition, or a more generous opportunity to present evidence.

Depending on the people, land, law, and context, Land Back may involve return of title, restoration of jurisdiction, co-governance, treaty fulfillment, access, stewardship authority, rematriation, protection of sacred sites, land transfer, or other nation-defined remedies.

ConstantC should not flatten those distinct claims into one slogan or one universal mechanism.

The constitutional point is that the remedy cannot be defined solely by the institution whose authority and property categories are themselves contested.

A hearing can be epistemically safe in a narrow sense while leaving the underlying dispossession untouched.

A process can believe the nation's testimony and still deny the nation's authority.

That is why standing and epistemic safety are necessary but insufficient here.

---

## Four different failures

### 1. Recognition failure

The settler system misrecognizes the Indigenous nation as a racial interest group, cultural community, or stakeholder rather than a political nation with its own law and authority.

The injury occurs before evidence weighting.

The system has constituted the participant incorrectly.

### 2. Standing failure

The nation is permitted to appear but is not entitled to matter within the domains it claims: treaty meaning, jurisdiction, law, stewardship, sacred relation, historical continuity, or remedy.

Its contribution may enter the record while being defined as advisory rather than authoritative or legally consequential.

### 3. Epistemic-safety failure

Policy formally recognizes the nation's evidence and role, but practice discounts oral history, Indigenous law, sacred knowledge, community testimony, or land-based knowledge compared with deeds, surveys, administrative records, and expert reports produced by settler institutions.

The nation has de jure standing but remains structurally discounted.

### 4. Sovereignty failure

The settler institution treats itself as the sole source of authority and the Indigenous nation as a participant inside a process the settler institution entirely owns.

The deeper political relationship—nation to nation, treaty partner, jurisdictional authority, or pre-existing peoplehood—is reduced to weighted input.

This is not repaired merely by granting more standing inside the same frame.

## Why this may be the stronger case

The prior-authorization case asks:

> Can the affected person matter inside a consequential inquiry?

The Indigenous land case asks a prior question:

> Who has authority to define the inquiry, the land, the governing law, and the relationship between the participants?

The first case isolates testimonial and procedural standing.

The second exposes **constitutive standing** and the limits of the concept itself.

If the adjudicating system defines the Indigenous nation, evidence, property, jurisdiction, and remedy before the nation enters the room, then the problem is not simply that testimony is discounted.

The room was built through dispossession and claims universal authority over questions it did not create.

---

## Standing versus recognition

Recognition may appear prior to standing:

> A system cannot allocate standing to a participant it refuses to encounter on the participant's own political and relational terms.

But recognition is dangerous when unilateral.

The settler state may claim the power to recognize, define, and withdraw recognition.

Recognition can therefore remain paternalistic unless it is constrained by treaty, law, reciprocal political relationship, and the nation's own authority.

Standing adds something recognition alone lacks:

> Recognition must carry a concrete entitlement for the nation's law, knowledge, and claim to alter the governing account.

Recognition without consequence is ceremony.

Standing without accurate recognition is allocation to a category imposed by someone else.

---

## Standing versus sovereignty

Sovereignty is not a larger amount of standing.

Standing operates inside an inquiry.

Sovereignty concerns authority to govern, define law, maintain political relationships, and exercise jurisdiction.

An Indigenous nation should not be flattened into one participant among many where its sovereign or treaty authority is legally and historically relevant.

A CDP participant-role map that assigns `Tribe: evidence source, weight 0.35` would reproduce the very harm under examination.

Where sovereignty exists, the architecture must represent authority, jurisdiction, and intergovernmental relationship—not merely voice or credibility.

The strongest surviving formulation is:

> **Within an inquiry, standing is the first covenant. Where sovereignty exists, the inquiry must first recognize that it did not create the authority it encounters.**

## Evidence and witness

This case requires faithful distinction.

Name what is:

- established by treaty or law;
- documented historically;
- measured environmentally or economically;
- testified by community members;
- carried through oral history or Indigenous knowledge;
- asserted as sacred, relational, or jurisdictional obligation;
- disputed;
- and not yet verified by the researcher.

Do not require Indigenous knowledge to become a Western scientific claim before it can matter.

Do not convert sacred or community knowledge into public evidence without consent.

Do not romanticize Indigenous knowledge or treat identity as automatic correctness.

Do not let the settler archive present itself as neutral merely because it is written, mapped, filed, or state-authenticated.

The evidence and witness must remain paired but distinct.

## A concrete hypothetical

An Indigenous nation seeks return or transfer of a parcel now controlled by a federal, state, municipal, university, church, corporate, or conservation institution.

The institution offers a formally contestable process.

It accepts:

- chain-of-title records;
- treaty documents;
- environmental evidence;
- oral history;
- archaeological reports;
- community testimony;
- and proposed stewardship plans.

But the institution defines the available remedies in advance as:

- a cultural-access agreement;
- an advisory role;
- a conservation easement;
- a commemorative marker;
- or a revocable co-management arrangement.

Full transfer, restored jurisdiction, or nation-defined governance is excluded before inquiry begins.

The nation may win every credibility question and still lose the constitutional question.

Its evidence is believed.

Its authority is not.

This is not merely epistemic unsafety.

It is a remedy and sovereignty foreclosure built into the scope of inquiry.

---

## Operational tests

A legitimate process should answer:

1. Who defined the inquiry and remedy set?
2. Was the Indigenous nation involved before scope was fixed?
3. In what capacity is the nation recognized: sovereign, treaty partner, rights holder, claimant, stakeholder, or evidence source?
4. Which law or laws govern, and who determined that?
5. Can the nation's own law and political authority alter the process, not merely the factual record?
6. Are oral history, Indigenous knowledge, sacred relation, stewardship practice, and community testimony admitted on terms that do not require destructive translation?
7. Who controls confidentiality and public disclosure?
8. Can the process produce return, jurisdiction, or authority, or only consultation?
9. Are conflicts created by the current title holder's control of the process independently reviewed?
10. What remedies are excluded in advance, and why?
11. Can the nation contest its assigned participant category?
12. Does a successful challenge change land control, jurisdiction, record, and future governance—or only the narrative?

## CDP implications

CDP must not flatten sovereign authority into generic participant standing.

A land-related decision record may require:

- nation and government identities;
- treaty and legal relationship;
- jurisdictional claims;
- authority source for each adjudicator;
- contested authority and conflict record;
- remedy scope and exclusions;
- consent and confidentiality boundaries;
- Indigenous-law and knowledge-handling protocols defined with the nation;
- provenance without forced public exposure;
- challenge by the nation to the process architecture itself;
- independent review of the current title holder's conflicts;
- and repair extending to title, jurisdiction, access, governance, record, and institutional practice.

The process may need intergovernmental or jointly governed adjudication rather than a single institution's ordinary Propose–Challenge–Adjudicate cycle.

CDP should represent that as a constitutional authority problem, not merely an evidence-weighting problem.

---

## Falsification and narrowing

This case should weaken the claim that standing is universally first if:

- sovereignty explains the constitutional injury more precisely and cannot be represented as domain-specific standing without loss;
- recognition of political status is logically required before any standing allocation can occur;
- justice or restitution grounds the remedy while standing only governs procedure;
- or the standing frame repeatedly converts nations into participants in a process whose legitimacy they contest.

The case supports a narrower claim:

> Standing may be first within an inquiry, but it is not necessarily prior to sovereignty, treaty relationship, jurisdiction, justice, or the political existence of peoples the inquiry did not create.

---

## What this case protects against

This case protects ConstantC from using a powerful Indigenous example merely to prove a favorite general theory.

The example may instead force the theory to become smaller and truer.

Land Back is not a metaphor.

Indigenous nations are not evidence categories.

Sovereignty is not a larger voice allocation.

A process does not become legitimate because the dispossessed are finally invited to describe what was taken.

---

## Closing

Standing asks whether a participant can matter inside inquiry.

Recognition asks whether the inquiry encounters the participant without forced misclassification.

Epistemic safety asks whether recognized standing survives practice.

Sovereignty asks whether the inquiry has mistaken another people's authority for an input into its own.

Justice asks what repair is due after dispossession has structured the field.

The land case does not crown one primitive.

It prevents a procedural vocabulary from becoming another map that declares itself the territory.
